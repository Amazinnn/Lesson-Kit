# Spacing Effect & Distributed Practice

## Core Claim

Distributing study sessions over time produces dramatically better long-term retention than massing them together (cramming). Some forgetting between sessions is desirable — it forces the brain to reconstruct the memory, strengthening it.

## Key Research

Ebbinghaus (1885) first documented the forgetting curve and spacing effect. Cepeda et al. (2006, 2008) found that optimal spacing gaps depend on the retention interval: for a 1-year retention goal, space practice 1-3 months apart. Recent 2025 research (Carpenter et al.) finds students are aware of spacing but don't use it due to perceived effort costs and the "massing bias" (massed practice creates an illusion of fluency).

## Effect Size & Confidence

- Spaced vs. massed practice: d = 0.60–0.90 (high confidence)
- Optimal gap = 10-20% of retention interval (moderate confidence)

## Boundary Conditions

- Spacing is more important for complex materials that require deeper processing.
- Very short intervals (minutes) between sessions provide minimal benefit.
- Learners consistently underestimate the value of spacing — metacognitive training may help.

## Practical Implications

- In 速览, the through-line perspective creates incidental spacing: a pattern seen in section A is referenced again in section C.
