# Motivation & Engagement

## Core Claim

Learner motivation is not a personality trait but a state shaped by the learning environment. Three key factors: (1) perceived value of the task, (2) expectation of success, and (3) the psychological needs for autonomy, competence, and relatedness.

## Key Research

Deci & Ryan (2000) proposed Self-Determination Theory (SDT), identifying autonomy, competence, and relatedness as universal psychological needs. Eccles & Wigfield (2002) developed Expectancy-Value Theory: motivation = expectation of success × perceived task value. Dweck (2006) showed that growth mindset (believing intelligence can develop) predicts persistence and achievement. Recent 2025 work (Carpenter et al., Cognitive Research) finds that students know effective strategies but avoid them due to perceived effort — a motivation problem, not a knowledge problem.

## Effect Size & Confidence

- Growth mindset interventions: d = 0.10–0.30 (small but replicable in meta-analyses)
- Autonomy-supportive teaching: d = 0.30–0.60 (medium-high confidence)
- Expectancy-value interventions: d = 0.30–0.50 (medium confidence)
- Intrinsic motivation interventions: d = 0.40–0.70 (medium-high confidence)

## Boundary Conditions

- Growth mindset interventions are most effective for low-performing or at-risk students.
- Expectancy-value effects vary by domain, age, and cultural context.
- Too much autonomy can overwhelm novice learners who need structure.
