# Schema Theory

## Core Claim

Knowledge is organized into mental structures called schemas. Learning is the process of building new schemas, connecting them to existing ones, and automating their retrieval. Experts differ from novices not in raw intelligence but in the quality and organization of their schemas.

## Key Research

Bartlett (1932) first proposed schemas. Sweller integrated schema theory into Cognitive Load Theory (1988), showing that schemas act as cognitive "chunks" that reduce working memory load. Recent neuroscience research confirms that schema-consistent information is encoded more rapidly and retained longer than schema-inconsistent information.

## Effect Size & Confidence

- Schema-based instruction vs. traditional: d = 0.60–1.00 (high confidence)
- Concept mapping (making schemas explicit): d = 0.50–0.80 (medium-high confidence)

## Boundary Conditions

- Schema construction takes time and repeated exposure — it cannot be rushed.
- Overly rigid schemas can hinder adaptation to new contexts (functional fixedness).
- Novices lack the schemas needed to benefit from advanced schema-based instruction.

## Practical Implications

- The knowledge-points structure (5-10 per section) mirrors schema construction: each KP is a schema element; the section is an integrated schema.
- The chapter overview gives students a "schema scaffold" before they encounter details.
- The through-line perspective helps students build cross-sectional schemas connecting multiple knowledge points.
- The kp_id naming system (course-source-ch03-kp-001) preserves schema structure for later knowledge graph construction.

## Connection to Other Theories

- **Cognitive Load Theory** (see fundamentals/cognitive-load-theory.md): Schemas are the mechanism for reducing cognitive load.
- **Prior Knowledge & Transfer** (see fundamentals/prior-knowledge-transfer.md): Schemas determine what prior knowledge can be activated and transferred.
