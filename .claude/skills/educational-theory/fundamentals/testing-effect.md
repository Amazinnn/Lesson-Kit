# Testing Effect (Retrieval Practice)

## Core Claim

Retrieving information from memory produces stronger long-term retention than restudying it. Every act of retrieval strengthens the neural pathway to the retrieved memory.

## Key Research

Roediger & Karpicke (2006) showed that students who took a test after reading recalled 50% more after one week than those who re-read. Recent 2025 studies confirm the effect in real primary school settings (Frontiers in Psychology, 2025), with complex materials (history texts), and with mobile learning apps (meta-analysis g = 0.88). A 2025 boundary study (Redifer et al., Instructional Science) found that retrieval practice with very complex materials increased cognitive load, which reduced its benefit — suggesting CLT bounds the testing effect.

## Effect Size & Confidence

- Testing effect vs. re-reading: d = 0.50–0.80 (high confidence)
- Testing with feedback vs. testing without: d = 0.40–0.60 (high confidence)
- Successive relearning (retrieval + spacing): d = 0.80–1.00 (high confidence)

## Boundary Conditions

- Testing without feedback loses much of its benefit — corrective feedback is essential.
- With highly complex materials, the retrieval attempt may overwhelm working memory — scaffold first.
- Multiple-choice testing is less effective than cued recall for retention, but more efficient for broad coverage.
