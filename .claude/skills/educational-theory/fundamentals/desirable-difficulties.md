# Desirable Difficulties

## Core Claim

Learning conditions that require effort — struggling, making errors, and persisting through difficulty — produce more durable long-term learning than conditions that feel easy and fluent.

## Key Research

Bjork & Bjork (1994) proposed the concept, later expanded by Bjork (2013). The core insight is that "difficulties" that slow acquisition but enhance long-term retention are "desirable." Recent 2025 work (Pyke, Lunau & Javadi) compares DDF with CLT, proposing an integrated model: difficulty should be calibrated to learner expertise and material complexity.

## Effect Size & Confidence

- Desirable difficulties (overall): d = 0.40–0.70 (medium-high confidence)
- Pretesting (attempt before study): d = 0.40–0.60 (medium-high from 2025 studies)
- Interleaving: d = 0.40–0.60 (medium-high confidence)

## Boundary Conditions

- Difficulties are only "desirable" if the learner can ultimately succeed with effort.
- For novices with very complex materials, desirable difficulties can become undesirable — the learner gets stuck without productive path forward.
- Feedback and scaffolding must be available to make difficulties productive rather than frustrating.

- The 速览's MCQ-first format is a desirable difficulty: the student must engage before getting the answer.
- The through-line perspective is a desirable difficulty for the designer: choosing a recurring pattern requires careful analysis, but produces a better learning experience.
