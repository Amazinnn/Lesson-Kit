# Cognitive Load Theory

## Core Claim

Working memory has limited capacity. Instruction should minimize extraneous cognitive load (how hard the material is to access) and optimize intrinsic load (how hard the material inherently is), freeing capacity for germane load (schema construction).

## Key Research

Sweller (1988) proposed CLT based on problem-solving research. Landmark application: the worked example effect (Sweller & Cooper, 1985) — novices learn more from studying solved problems than from solving problems themselves. Recent 2025 work (Pyke, Lunau & Javadi) integrates CLT with Desirable Difficulties Framework: task difficulty should be adjusted based on material complexity and learner expertise.

## Effect Size & Confidence

- Worked examples vs. problem-solving: d = 0.50–1.00 (high confidence)
- Split-attention effect: d = 0.70–1.20 (high confidence)
- Modality effect (audio+visual vs. visual only): d = 0.60–1.00 (high confidence)

## Boundary Conditions

- CLT effects are strongest for novices; experts may experience the expertise reversal effect (see aha-moments/expertise-reversal.md).
- Element interactivity matters: low-element-interactivity content may not benefit from CLT-based design.
- The three load types (intrinsic/extraneous/germane) are difficult to measure independently.

## Practical Implications

- 速览知识 points should each be a single, coherent schema — not multiple interacting elements.
- The clean definition + through-line format minimizes extraneous load (no extra words, no narrative padding).
- MCQ questions should test one dimension at a time, not multiple interacting conditions.

## Connection to Other Theories

- Complements **Desirable Difficulties** (see fundamentals/desirable-difficulties.md): CLT says reduce difficulty for novices; DDF says increase it. The resolution: difficulty should increase as expertise grows.
- Works through **Schema Theory** (see fundamentals/schema-theory.md): schemas are the mechanism by which cognitive load is managed.
