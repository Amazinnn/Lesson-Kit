# Prior Knowledge & Transfer

## Core Claim

Learning builds on prior knowledge. What students already know — accurate or not — determines how they interpret, encode, and retrieve new information. Transfer (applying knowledge in new contexts) is the ultimate goal of learning but does not happen automatically.

## Key Research

Ambrose et al. (2010) identifies prior knowledge as the first of seven principles: it can help or hinder learning depending on accuracy, activation, and organization. Bransford et al. (2000, How People Learn) shows that expert knowledge is organized around core principles, not surface features — this is what enables transfer. Recent work emphasizes that activating prior knowledge before instruction improves encoding by providing a "scaffold" for new information.

## Effect Size & Confidence

- Prior knowledge activation: d = 0.40–0.70 (high confidence)
- Transfer-focused instruction: d = 0.40–0.80 (medium-high confidence — highly variable)
- Misconception correction: d = 0.40–0.60 (medium-high confidence)

## Boundary Conditions

- Activated but incorrect prior knowledge can interfere with learning — misconceptions must be addressed directly.
- Transfer depends on the similarity between learning and transfer contexts (near vs. far transfer).
- Novices benefit more from explicit instruction on when to apply knowledge than from discovery approaches.

## Practical Implications

- The chapter overview activates prior knowledge by showing where this chapter fits.
- The dependency_kp_ids in knowledge-points.md document prior knowledge prerequisites.
- The through-line perspective leverages prior knowledge from earlier sections to scaffold later ones.
- When a student gets an MCQ wrong, the error often traces to a prior-knowledge gap or misconception — the answer explanation should point to where in the source the gap is addressed.

## Connection to Other Theories

- **Schema Theory** (see fundamentals/schema-theory.md): Prior knowledge IS existing schemas; new learning modifies them.
- **Cognitive Load Theory** (see fundamentals/cognitive-load-theory.md): Activated prior knowledge reduces intrinsic load by providing a pre-existing schema.
- **ICAP** (see fundamentals/icap-framework.md): Constructive engagement requires connecting new information to prior knowledge.
