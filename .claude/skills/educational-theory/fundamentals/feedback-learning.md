# Feedback for Learning

## Core Claim

Feedback is one of the most powerful influences on learning — but its effectiveness depends on type, timing, and specificity. Task-level feedback (is this right?) is the minimum; process-level feedback (how to improve) and self-regulation feedback (how to monitor your own learning) are more powerful.

## Key Research

Hattie & Timperley (2007) proposed the feedback model with four levels: task, process, self-regulation, and self (ineffective). Hattie's updated Visible Learning: The Sequel (2023) synthesizes over 2,100 meta-analyses, confirming feedback as one of the highest-impact influences (d ~0.70-0.80). Recent work emphasizes that feedback must be received and acted upon to have impact — simply giving feedback is not enough.

## Effect Size & Confidence

- Overall feedback: d = 0.70 (high confidence — but highly variable)
- Task feedback: d = 0.40–0.60
- Process feedback: d = 0.60–0.80
- Self-regulation feedback: d = 0.70–0.90
- Praise (self-level): d = 0.10–0.20 (low effectiveness)

## Boundary Conditions

- Feedback is most effective when the learner can use it to improve immediately.
- Delayed feedback can be more effective than immediate feedback for retention (desirable difficulty).
- Feedback that threatens self-esteem (person-level criticism) is actively harmful.

## Practical Implications

- Section-end answers in 速览 provide immediate task-level feedback + brief process-level guidance (the one-sentence explanation).
- The answer explanation should focus on "where to look in the source" (process) rather than just "which answer is right" (task).
- In later stages, design feedback that requires the student to act: "Your answer was X. Re-read section Y to find why. Then try question Z."

## Connection to Other Theories

- **Error-Driven Learning** (see fundamentals/desirable-difficulties.md): Feedback is the mechanism by which errors drive learning.
- **Testing Effect** (see fundamentals/testing-effect.md): Retrieval practice + feedback is dramatically more effective than retrieval practice alone.
