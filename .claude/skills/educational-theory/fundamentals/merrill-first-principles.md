# Merrill's First Principles of Instruction

## Core Claim

Regardless of instructional approach, five principles consistently appear in effective instruction: learning is facilitated when learners (1) solve real-world problems, (2) activate prior knowledge, (3) see demonstrations, (4) apply new knowledge, and (5) integrate it into their lives.

## Key Research

Merrill (2002) proposed the five principles based on a review of major instructional design theories. Recent 2024 evidence (Sarfi et al., BMC Medical Education) shows that FPI-designed training increased knowledge scores by 42% (d = 0.9, p < .001) compared to standard training, with effects sustained at 2-week follow-up. A 2024 dissertation (Kiech, U. Memphis) found that most online STEM courses integrate FPI at low levels — a gap between research and practice.

## Effect Size & Confidence

- FPI-based design vs. traditional: d = 0.50–0.90 (medium-high confidence — limited studies)
- Problem-centered instruction: d = 0.40–0.80 (medium-high confidence)

## Boundary Conditions

- FPI is a meta-framework rather than a specific theory — its principles are broad and implementation varies.
- Some principles (e.g., integration) are easier to apply in later learning stages than initial exposure.
- The real-world problem principle may conflict with 速览's "don't require calculation" constraint.
