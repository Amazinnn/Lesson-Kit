# Metacognition & Self-Regulation

## Core Claim

Learning improves dramatically when students can monitor their own understanding, plan their approach, and adjust strategies based on results. Yet most students — even high-performing ones — lack these skills and need explicit instruction.

## Key Research

Flavell (1979) introduced metacognition. Ambrose et al. (2010, How Learning Works) identifies self-regulation as one of seven core learning principles. Schraw (1998) found that metacognitive training improves performance across domains. Hattie's 2023 Visible Learning update reports metacognition effect size of d ~0.69.

## Effect Size & Confidence

- Metacognitive training: d = 0.50–0.70 (high confidence)
- Self-explanation: d = 0.40–0.70 (medium-high confidence)
- Exam wrappers/reflection prompts: d = 0.30–0.50 (medium confidence)

## Boundary Conditions

- Young or low-performing students may need more scaffolding before metacognitive strategies are effective.
- Metacognitive training must be domain-specific to transfer.
- Overconfidence (Dunning-Kruger) is particularly resistant to metacognitive correction unless feedback is timely and specific.

## Practical Implications

- The 速览's MCQ + answer structure provides external feedback that calibrates self-assessment: "I thought I knew that, but I got it wrong."
- The "return to source" requirement forces metacognitive monitoring: "I got it wrong, so I need to re-read section X."
- For later stages, explicitly include reflection prompts: "What did you get wrong? Why? What will you do differently?"
- The section-end answers serve as low-stakes calibration — teaching students to distinguish fluency from mastery.

## Connection to Other Theories

- **Illusions of Knowing** (see aha-moments/illusions-of-knowing.md): Metacognitive failure is the root cause of illusions of knowing.
- **Testing Effect** (see fundamentals/testing-effect.md): Testing provides the metacognitive calibration needed to overcome illusions of knowing.
