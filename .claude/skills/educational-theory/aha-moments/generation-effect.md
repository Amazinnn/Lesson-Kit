# The Generation Effect

Generating an answer (even a wrong one) produces better long-term retention than reading the correct answer. The act of retrieval itself strengthens memory, regardless of whether the retrieval is successful.

## Why This Matters

This is the core mechanism behind why pretesting works: attempting a question before knowing the answer encodes the gap in memory, so when you later encounter the correct answer, it's more salient and better retained. It also explains why "try to solve before being shown" is more effective than "watch then try."

## Source

Slamecka & Graf (1978). The generation effect. *Journal of Experimental Psychology: Human Learning and Memory*, 4(6), 592–604.

## How It Connects

Underpins both **retrieval practice** (see fundamentals/testing-effect.md) and **productive failure** (see aha-moments/productive-failure.md). Also explains why the quick_absorption MCQ design — where students must answer before receiving confirmation — is more effective than reading an explanation.
