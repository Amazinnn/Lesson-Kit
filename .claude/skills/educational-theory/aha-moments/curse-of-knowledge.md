# The Curse of Knowledge

Once you know something well, you can no longer imagine what it's like not to know it. This makes it nearly impossible to predict which parts of your material are confusing to a learner.

## Why This Matters

Every instructional designer suffers from this. You assume certain connections are "obvious" because you already see them. The student doesn't. This is why beginners need concrete examples, explicit scaffolding, and clearly stated conditions — things experts find "tedious" but novices find essential.

## Source

Camerer, Loewenstein & Weber (1989). The curse of knowledge in economic settings. *Journal of Political Economy*.

## How It Connects

Directly explains the **expertise reversal effect** (see fundamentals/desirable-difficulties.md) — what helps novices hinders experts and vice versa. Also relates to why quick_absorption must avoid "just explain it" — you think you're clarifying, but you're actually skipping the hard parts.
